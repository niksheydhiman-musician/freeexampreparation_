Why the Drawing Section Decides So Much

NATA tests your aptitude for architecture through two parts: a drawing test and a multiple choice section covering math, general aptitude, and visual reasoning. Most students prepare hard for the objective section because it feels measurable. You practice fifty questions, you get a score, you know where you stand.

The drawing section feels different. There's no answer key. You can't tell if your sketch is "right." That uncertainty makes students under-prepare for the one section that often separates a good NATA score from an average one.

Here's the part that should change how you practice: the mistakes that cost marks in the drawing test are rarely about talent. They're about habits. A student with average drawing skill who avoids these ten mistakes will consistently outscore a more talented student who repeats them. Let's go through each one.

1

Drawing Too Small

The mistake

You're given a full A4 or A3 sheet, and your composition takes up a quarter of it. The rest sits empty, or gets filled with random doodles that have nothing to do with the question.

This happens because students draw cautiously when they're nervous. A small drawing feels safer because there's less to get wrong. But examiners read a small composition as weak confidence, even when the actual drawing skill is fine.

The fix

Before you draw a single line, lightly mark the boundary of your composition using faint pencil guides that use at least 80 percent of the page. Practice this at home on actual A3 sheets, not in a small notebook. Your hand needs to learn what "big" feels like so it doesn't shrink under exam pressure.

2

Ignoring the Question Word by Word

The mistake

NATA questions are specific. "Draw a marketplace scene during the evening with at least three human figures and one vehicle" has four separate requirements. Students read the first half, get an idea, and start drawing before finishing the sentence. The vehicle never makes it onto the page.

Every missing requirement is marks left on the table, and it has nothing to do with how well you can draw.

The fix

Read the question twice. The first time for understanding, the second time with a pencil, underlining every concrete requirement: the setting, the time of day, the number of figures, any specific objects mentioned. Tick each one off mentally as you include it in your composition. This takes thirty seconds and saves you from the most avoidable mistake on this entire list.

3

Weak Perspective and Proportion

The mistake

Buildings lean at odd angles. A person standing next to a door is taller than the doorframe. A car looks bigger than the building behind it. These errors are common because perspective is the one skill students assume they'll "figure out" during the exam instead of practicing beforehand.

Architecture programs care deeply about spatial reasoning. A drawing with shaky perspective signals to the examiner that you might struggle with the spatial thinking architecture school demands, regardless of how creative your idea is.

The fix

Spend two weeks doing nothing but one-point and two-point perspective drills. Draw simple boxes, then buildings, then street scenes using visible vanishing points and horizon lines. Once perspective becomes automatic, you stop thinking about it during the exam and your focus shifts to the actual composition.

4

Overworking One Part of the Sheet

The mistake

You spend forty minutes perfecting one building in the foreground, rendering every brick and window with care, then realize you have ten minutes left and the rest of the page is barely sketched.

A gorgeous corner on an otherwise unfinished page scores lower than a complete composition rendered evenly. Examiners assess the whole sheet, not your best square inch.

The fix

Block out your entire composition in light pencil first, every element in the scene, before you commit to detailed rendering on any single part. Only after the full layout exists should you go back and add depth and detail, working evenly across the page rather than finishing one section completely before starting the next.

5

Messy or Inconsistent Line Work

The mistake

Some lines are drawn confidently in one stroke. Others are scratchy, redrawn three or four times, visibly hesitant. The inconsistency makes the whole sheet look unplanned, even if the actual content is solid.

This usually comes from drawing without warming up. Cold hands produce shaky lines, especially in the first ten minutes of any drawing session.

The fix

Before every practice session, and before the actual exam if you can manage it, spend five minutes drawing loose lines and basic shapes just to warm up your hand. On the exam sheet itself, commit to each line. A confidently drawn line that's slightly off is better than a hesitant line redrawn five times to get it "right."

6

Leaving Shading and Rendering Incomplete

The mistake

The line drawing is solid, but there's no shading at all, or it's added in a rush during the last two minutes, with a few random scribbles that don't follow any light source.

Shading is what turns a flat line drawing into something with depth and atmosphere. Skipping it, or doing it carelessly, leaves marks on the table in the rendering and presentation part of your score.

The fix

Decide on a light source before you start shading anything, even something as simple as "light from the top left." Shade consistently based on that decision across every object in the scene. Budget actual time for this in your practice sessions instead of treating it as an afterthought you'll get to if time allows.

7

Forgetting Scale and Human Reference

The mistake

A composition full of buildings, streets, and objects, but no human figures anywhere. Without people in the scene, there's nothing to tell the viewer how big anything actually is.

Architecture is built for people. A scene without any human presence misses one of the most basic things the examiner is checking for: do you understand how humans inhabit the spaces you're drawing.

The fix

Make it a rule for yourself: every composition includes at least two or three human figures, placed naturally within the scene rather than stuck awkwardly in a corner. Practice quick figure sketches separately so you can place them confidently without slowing down your main composition.

8

Poor Time Management Across Questions

The mistake

The drawing test usually has two questions to complete in ninety minutes. Students get absorbed in the first question, lose track of time, and rush the second one badly, or don't finish it at all.

An unfinished second drawing almost always costs more marks than a slightly less polished first one. Time mismanagement is one of the most preventable reasons for a low drawing score.

The fix

Decide your time split before you start. Roughly forty minutes for the first question and forty minutes for the second, with ten minutes kept as a buffer for finishing touches. Wear a watch and check it at fixed checkpoints, not just when you happen to remember. Practice this exact split during your mock sessions so it becomes automatic.

9

Copying Memorised Templates

The mistake

Some students prepare three or four "safe" compositions, a market scene, a park scene, a street scene, and try to force whatever the exam question is into one of those templates, regardless of fit.

Examiners see this constantly. A forced, generic composition that doesn't actually match the question reads as exactly what it is: a memorised drawing, not a genuine response to the prompt.

The fix

Practice with a wide range of prompts instead of perfecting a handful of fixed scenes. Train your ability to interpret a new question quickly and sketch a composition that actually fits it, rather than training yourself to reproduce the same drawing on command. Flexibility scores better than polish on the wrong idea.

10

A Messy, Unlabelled Final Sheet

The mistake

Construction lines from your initial layout are still visible and never erased. There's no title, no labels for the elements in the scene where the question expects them. The sheet looks unfinished even when the actual content is decent.

Presentation is part of the score, and a cluttered final sheet undersells work that might otherwise be strong.

The fix

Keep the last five minutes of your time strictly for cleanup: erase stray guidelines, darken the lines you want to keep, and add a title or label if the question calls for one. Build this cleanup time into your time budget from the start so it never gets sacrificed when you're running late.

A Simple Way to Fix All of This

You don't need to fix all ten mistakes overnight. Pick the two or three that show up most often in your own practice sheets and focus there first. Most students find that fixing time management and composition size alone improves their drawing scores noticeably within two or three weeks.

The best way to catch these mistakes is to treat every practice sketch like a real exam sheet. Set a timer. Use a full page. Read the prompt twice. Then look at your finished sheet with the list above next to it and check, honestly, which mistakes are still showing up. That single habit, repeated weekly, does more for your score than any amount of talent.

One habit worth building now: keep a simple practice log. After each sketch, write down the one mistake you noticed and the one thing you'll do differently next time. Three weeks of this turns into a clear, visible improvement curve, and it shows you exactly what to focus on in the final stretch before the exam.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does the drawing section actually weigh in NATA?

The drawing test typically carries significant weight in the overall NATA score, alongside the multiple choice section covering mathematics and general aptitude. Check the latest official NATA brochure for the exact weightage for 2026, since the split has been revised in past years.

Can I improve my drawing score in a short time before the exam?

Yes, more than most students expect. Drawing skill takes months to build from scratch, but the ten mistakes covered here are habits, not skills. Fixing your time management, reading the question properly, and using the full page can improve your score within a few weeks, even if your raw drawing ability stays the same.

What should I carry for the NATA drawing test?

Pencils of varying grades, a good eraser, a sharpener, and a scale or ruler if permitted. Check the current NATA information bulletin for the exact list of allowed materials, since this is updated for every cycle and strict at the exam center.

Is it okay to use color in the NATA drawing section?

NATA's drawing test generally focuses on pencil sketching rather than color rendering. Confirm this against the official 2026 exam pattern before assuming, since formats have varied slightly across years.

Should I practice drawing the same way every day, or mix it up?

Mix it up. Spend some sessions on technical drills like perspective and proportion, and other sessions on full timed compositions with fresh, unfamiliar prompts. Drilling the same scene repeatedly builds memorised templates, which is mistake nine on this list.

Final Word

None of these ten mistakes require natural talent to fix. They require attention, a bit of self review, and consistent practice under exam-like conditions. Go through your last few practice sheets right now and check how many of these you can spot. That single exercise will tell you exactly where to focus for the next few weeks.

Good luck with your preparation.
